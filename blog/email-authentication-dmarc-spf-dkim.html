DMARC, SPF & DKIM: Email Security for Australian Small Business

Stop worrying about whether your emails are landing in spam — or worse, whether someone else is sending them.

DMARC SPF DKIM Email Security Phishing Small Business Cybersecurity

The Problem

Every day, millions of emails are sent pretending to be from domains they don't belong to. Your domain. Your customers' trust. Your brand's reputation — all at risk, and you might not even know it.

Email was built in an era of trust. That era is over. Today, anyone with basic technical knowledge can forge a "From" address and send email that looks like it came from you. Without the right protections in place, your customers, suppliers, and partners have no way of telling the difference between a legitimate email from your business and a carefully crafted fake.

The consequences? Phishing attacks bearing your name. Fake invoices sent to your clients. Your domain blacklisted, your legitimate emails quarantined, and your brand's integrity eroded — one spoofed message at a time.

Three protocols exist to stop this: DMARC, SPF, and DKIM. Together, they give receiving mail servers cryptographic proof that an email is genuinely from you. And for Australian small businesses, implementing them isn't just good security practice — it's increasingly a requirement.

Why This Matters Now

Major email providers — Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo — have been tightening their requirements for years. Domains without DMARC enforcement are increasingly flagged, quarantined, or rejected outright.

For Australian businesses, the stakes are higher still:

  • The ACSC (Australian Cyber Security Centre) recommends DMARC enforcement as a baseline security control
  • A successful phishing attack exploiting your domain can trigger obligations under the Privacy Act
  • ASIC and ATO communications must remain deliverable — no room for error

The question isn't whether you can afford to set this up. It's whether you can afford not to.

The Three Protocols That Protect Your Domain

SPF — Who's Allowed to Send

SPF is your domain's guest list. It publishes a simple record in your DNS that tells the world: "These IP addresses are authorised to send email on our behalf. Everyone else is an imposter."

yourdomain.com  TXT  "v=spf1 include:_spf.google.com ip4:203.0.113.0/24 ~all"
  • include:_spf.google.com — authorises Google Workspace
  • ip4:203.0.113.0/24 — authorises your office IP range
  • ~all — flags unauthorised senders (or use -all for hard rejection)

It sounds simple, but misconfigurations are common. Every third-party service you use — Mailchimp, Campaign Monitor, SendGrid, Salesforce — needs to be included. Miss one, and their emails fail authentication.

DKIM — Tamper-Proof Signatures

While SPF checks who sent it, DKIM checks is it real. Each email is signed with a private key held by your email provider. Receiving servers verify that signature against a public key published in your DNS.

google._domainkey.yourdomain.com  TXT  "v=DKIM1; k=rsa; p=MIGfMA0GCSqGSIb3..."

Even if an attacker spoofs the From address, they cannot forge the DKIM signature — they don't hold the private key. The maths simply doesn't work in their favour.

DMARC — The Policy Enforcer

DMARC ties SPF and DKIM together and tells receiving servers: "If these checks fail, here's exactly what to do — and here's where to send the evidence."

_dmarc.yourdomain.com  TXT  "v=DMARC1; p=quarantine; rua=mailto:dmarc@yourdomain.com; pct=100"
  • p=quarantine — deliver suspicious emails to spam
  • p=reject — block them outright
  • rua= — receive daily XML reports showing who's sending email as you
  • ruf= — receive detailed forensic reports on individual failures

DMARC also gives you something uniquely valuable: visibility. Those aggregate reports show every service and server attempting to send email as your domain — including ones you've forgotten about, or ones you never authorised at all.

A Strategy, Not a Toggle

DMARC enforcement isn't something you flip on overnight. The recommended progression is deliberate and data-driven:

  1. Monitor (p=none). Deploy DMARC in reporting-only mode for 2–4 weeks. Collect data on everything sending email as your domain — the authorised, the unauthorised, and the surprising.
  2. Quarantine (p=quarantine). Once you understand your landscape, begin routing authentication failures to spam. Watch for false positives.
  3. Reject (p=reject). Only move to hard rejection when you're confident every legitimate sender is authenticated. This is the gold standard — and increasingly, the expectation.

Each stage is safer than the last, and each is informed by the data gathered before it.

The Catch: SPF's 10-Lookup Limit

There's one limitation that catches growing businesses off guard. SPF records are limited to 10 DNS lookups. Every include: statement counts as one, and it adds up fast:

  • Google Workspace: 1 lookup
  • Mailchimp: 1 lookup
  • SendGrid: 1 lookup
  • Salesforce: 1 lookup
  • HubSpot: 1 lookup

Five services, and you've already consumed half your allowance. Many mid-sized businesses hit the ceiling without realising it — at which point SPF silently fails, and DMARC enforcement starts breaking legitimate email.

How enterprises solve this: SPF macro and flattening services — offered by providers like Valimail, DMARCLY, PowerDMARC, and EasyDMARC — automatically resolve all include: statements to a flat list of IP addresses. The flattened record contains only ip4: and ip6: entries, which don't count toward the lookup limit. Some use SPF macros (the % macro language defined in RFC 7208) to dynamically construct lookups based on who's sending. Others maintain a continuously updated, compressed SPF record that stays well under the limit.

For most Australian small businesses, this isn't a concern yet. But if you're growing, it's worth knowing about before the limit becomes a crisis.

Setup Checklist

For a typical small business on Google Workspace:

  1. SPF — Publish a TXT record listing Google's SPF include + any other legitimate senders
  2. DKIM — Enable DKIM signing in Google Workspace admin, publish the public key
  3. DMARC — Start with p=none, add a report address, monitor for 2 weeks
  4. Review — Check the reports for unauthorised senders or gaps
  5. Progress — Move to p=quarantine, then p=reject
  6. Maintain — Review reports monthly for new or forgotten senders

A note for Australian businesses: DNS providers like VentraIP, Netregistry, and Crazy Domains don't always make SPF and DKIM configuration straightforward. You may need to add DNS records manually — which is precisely where small mistakes can cause big problems.

The Bottom Line

Email authentication isn't complex, but it is precise. A missing record, a forgotten service, or an exceeded lookup limit can undo everything. The difference between security theatre and real protection is in the detail.
